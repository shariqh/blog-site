---
title: My mistake on diversity and inclusion
date: 2026-06-03
summary: I thought rigorous hiring and a strong team culture were enough. The mistake was using "culture fit" as a filter without examining what it was actually selecting out, and then assuming inclusion would take care of itself once someone joined.
tags: [leadership, culture, teams]
---

The mistake wasn't obvious at the time. Most real ones aren't.

Early in managing a small engineering team, I was proud of our hiring process. We were deliberate. We wrote real rubrics, calibrated our interviewers, and debriefed seriously. The bar felt high in the right ways.

And we had a final check: "culture fit." Did the candidate feel like someone who'd slot into the team, communicate the way we communicated, ramp without friction?

I told myself this was protecting quality. I think I was mostly protecting familiarity.

The filter did work I never examined. When someone cleared every technical round but a few interviewers came out of the debrief unsure, I had language for that. When a strong candidate felt "off" in the final conversation, I had a bucket. Culture fit. I used it like it was precise.

It wasn't. Looking back at the candidates we passed on with that label, I can see the pattern. We weren't filtering for clarity of thought or collaboration instinct or delivery track record; we had separate signals for all of those. "Culture fit" was doing something else. It was selecting for people who already looked and sounded like us.

**That's the mistake.** Not malice. The failure mode was much quieter. I genuinely thought rigor and good intentions were enough. What I missed is that a hiring process full of good intentions can still encode bad assumptions when you don't examine the criteria themselves.

The "fit" framing is specifically where a lot of that slips through. It sounds like a real thing because it is a real thing: teams do need some shared norms, communication styles, ways of working. But "culture fit" as a catch-all gives interviewers a socially acceptable way to express discomfort with difference. Nobody says "this person isn't like us." They say the culture fit felt off.

I didn't catch that distinction for longer than I should have.

---

The second mistake was subtler: thinking that hiring was where the work lived.

Even after I started scrutinizing "fit" criteria more carefully, I assumed that once someone joined, the environment would take care of itself. We were a good team. We were collegial. People liked working there.

But inclusion isn't an environment you build once and maintain. It's something you actively do. Who gets asked their opinion in a design review? Whose instincts does the team treat as worth following? Who gets the early feedback that lets someone course-correct before a review cycle? When a new person joins from a background that doesn't match the existing team's, the informal mentorship network doesn't just extend to them automatically. You have to build it.

I wasn't building it. I was being passive, assuming the culture would be self-distributing. For some people it was. For others, it wasn't.

A teammate pointed this out to me eventually, not bluntly, just by asking whether we'd thought about onboarding differently for people who didn't have prior relationships with anyone on the team. That was the observation I should have made myself, six months earlier.

---

There isn't a clean arc here where I fixed everything. This is a record of two specific things I got wrong, what the pattern was, and what I've changed.

On hiring: "culture fit" is gone from my rubrics. What replaced it is less elegant but more honest: a set of specific behaviors I actually care about (how does this person handle disagreement? how do they communicate uncertainty? how have they brought others along?) and explicit acknowledgment that "this person is different from the team" is not a disqualifier. Different is frequently an asset.

On inclusion: I run more explicit 1:1 conversations now about how people are experiencing the team, not just their project work. It's closer to: are you getting the information you need? Are you in the rooms where decisions get made? What would make this easier? The signal from that is very different from assuming everything is fine because nobody's complaining.

Neither of these is a breakthrough. They're table stakes for managing well. I'm writing this down because it took me longer than it should have to see what was actually happening, and because "culture fit" has enough defenders in engineering culture that it's worth being specific about what it was doing.

The concrete reframe: fit is mostly about the interviewer, not the candidate. When you feel like someone fits, ask what specifically you're measuring. If the answer is vague, the work isn't done.
