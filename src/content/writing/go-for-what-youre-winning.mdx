The responsible career advice goes like this: identify your weaknesses, address them, become well-rounded. It's what sounds humble and thoughtful. It gets written in annual reviews and repeated in 1:1s.

I followed it for a long time before I started questioning whether it was actually true.

The pattern I keep noticing, across people I've worked with and decisions I've watched play out, is almost the inverse. The moves that compounded weren't the ones where someone shored up a weakness. They were the ones where someone identified what was already working and went harder at it.

Not "what are you good at" — that's a different question. "What are you winning at?" Where is your effort converting to results at an unusually good rate? Where are other people finding value before you've even tried to deliver it?

Those two questions point to different places.

## The weakness trap

Here's what happens when you optimize for weaknesses: even if you improve, you usually end up average at the thing. And average at a weakness doesn't compound. You've spent time and energy going from below-average to forgettable. The people who were already good at that thing are still ahead of you, and they were spending that same time going deeper on something they were already winning at.

The calculus changes when you ask what happens if you redirect that investment toward a strength. Average becomes above-average. Above-average becomes excellent. Excellent, combined with a reputation that's had time to settle, becomes the person everyone calls when that specific problem matters.

I've watched engineers who were exceptional at debugging and performance build quiet, durable careers. Not because they were well-rounded. Some were openly average at product sense or written communication. But when a production system was melting down, everyone knew who to call. That reputation had weight. They went deeper on what they were already winning at, and the depth became the value.

## The signal problem

The harder part is reading the signal correctly.

What you're winning at isn't always obvious, especially early. The feedback loop is slow. You might be getting outsized results from something you treat as incidental: a way you explain systems, a pattern you use when debugging, a particular way you work through conflict on a team. These get ignored precisely because they feel natural and effortless. That ease is the signal.

The weakness, by contrast, is loud. It shows up in feedback, in awkward moments, in comparisons. It demands attention. So attention goes there.

The reframe is to treat what feels effortless as a data point, not as something beneath notice. If results are coming back better than expected, if people return to you for the same type of help repeatedly, if you find yourself solving something in ten minutes that seems to take others an hour — that's the signal. That's what to run toward.

## The practical version

This isn't an argument for ignoring every weakness forever. Some gaps are genuinely blocking. If a communication issue is costing you relationships or opportunities, it needs enough attention to stop being actively harmful. But "stop being harmful" is a much lower bar than "become excellent at this." Clear it, then move on.

The energy that's left goes to what's already working. Find the contexts where that thing gets used. Make yourself easier to find when that problem comes up. Go deeper on the craft itself.

The question I've started asking when something feels like it's going well: what if I took this more seriously? Not as a side effect of other work, but as the main bet.

Most of the time the answer is that nobody has, because the advice we all got was to go fix something else.
