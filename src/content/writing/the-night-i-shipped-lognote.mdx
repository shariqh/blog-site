I have a README line I almost deleted three times: "Early but usable. A fast-moving beta, not a finished product." I kept it. Last night, around 6:45pm PT, I sent the private-beta announcement to the waitlist and let people start cloning the repo.

lognote is the thing I have wanted to exist for two years. It records a meeting from your mic and your system audio through a CoreAudio process tap, transcribes it on-device with WhisperKit on the Neural Engine, summarizes it with a local model by default, and drops a clean note (TL;DR, action items, decisions, open questions) into Obsidian. No DMG. No App Store. The repo is still private. Just `git clone` and `./setup.sh`, sent to people who raised a hand. This was an invite, not a parade.

I expected to feel proud. Mostly I felt exposed. I had braced for the product to break. I had not braced for the plumbing.

## Thirteen minutes

The announcement went live. Thirteen minutes later I was pushing a hotfix. Not to the app. To the website.

The launch post built clean and uploaded clean, and then it 404'd on lognote.dev. Every time. The site had been quietly deploying to a Cloudflare preview alias the entire time, never to production, because `wrangler-action` never expanded the deploy-branch variable. So every green checkmark I had trusted for weeks told the truth about a URL nobody could load. The one page I most wanted people to see went live at an address only I could reach.

It worked. It just had not moved. I fixed the variable, redeployed, watched the real domain resolve, and laughed the laugh of someone who just learned their victory lap had been run in an empty stadium.

## The 64-gigabyte afternoon

A tester imported a 45-minute Teams transcript and froze their Mac. Hard. The kind of freeze where the fans spin up and you start apologizing to the laptop.

Here is what happened, in order, because the order is the whole joke. The Import button had no in-flight guard. A stale onboarding modal still promised "Azure summarize takes ~5s," so a first run that took longer simply looked frozen. Looking frozen invites clicking. So they clicked again, and again. Each click spawned another engine process, each one loaded its own 8GB model in parallel, and macOS rolled the whole pile up under Obsidian, which proudly reported around 64GB in use. The machine did exactly what it was told. It was told something ridiculous. I built the trap, then mislabeled it as safe.

Five hours of focused work later it was gone. A cross-process model-load lock so two engines can never race for the same memory. A RAM gate that picks the largest model that actually fits the machine in front of it, not the largest model I wish everyone had. An in-flight guard that greys the button the instant you press it, so impatience does nothing instead of everything. The OOM was a stack of small honest gaps lined up end to end, the most satisfying kind of bug to close: every fix is obviously correct once you see the row.

The detail I keep holding onto: the recording and transcription path, the heart of the product, never flinched. Not once, all day. The mic and system-audio tap held. WhisperKit kept doing its job on device. The worst failure came from Import, the newest thing I bolted on last. The core held while the edges caught fire, and that told me the foundation is sound.

There were smaller fires too. Notifications had been silently broken for every alpha tester, because onboarding never requested permission, so every "notes ready" banner had been firing into the void. For testers running obsidian-git, a `git clean` quietly deleted the plugin files while my walkthrough still showed a cheerful green "installed" checkmark. And mid-afternoon, the private repo's GitHub Actions ran out of free minutes and plan-blocked CI at the exact hour I needed it most, fixes queued and nowhere to run them. Merging is publishing, and for a few tense hours I could not merge anything. I moved CI to a self-hosted runner, topped up credits, and got the pipeline breathing again inside the hour. Launch day is precisely when you burn through whatever runway your tooling quietly assumed you had.

## Why ship it rough

I could have waited. Guarded that Import button first, written the permission prompt, caught the preview-alias bug in a staging pass nobody would have applauded. Polished-and-waiting is a comfortable place to live. It is also a place where nothing ever moves.

Here is the take I keep committing to: honest-and-shipping beats polished-and-waiting. Not because rough is good, but because rough in front of real people is the only thing that turns into not-rough. The 64GB freeze is now a model-load lock and a RAM gate that protect every tester who comes after, and that fix does not exist if I keep the repo to myself another month. The bug had to meet a real machine before I could kill it.

Day one is over. The core records, transcribes, and summarizes, and it did all day without a stumble. The edges are getting sturdier by the hour. Next I am writing the onboarding permission flow and the import guards into the setup itself, so the next person's day one is calmer than mine. I will write that post after it actually runs.
